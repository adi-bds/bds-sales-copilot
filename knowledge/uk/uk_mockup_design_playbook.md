# BDS UK Sales — Mockup Design & Artwork Approval Playbook

**Based on:** Analysis of 150 real email threads covering 2D mockup requests, artwork submissions, design approvals, and file requirements  
**Reps featured:** Ross (Manager), Kaviya (Sales Manager), Aaron, Jenny, Jennifer, Tara, Gladina, Manusha, Arfath, Natlie, Lucus  
**Last updated:** April 2026

---

## Section 1: The Standard BDS UK Mockup Process

This is the end-to-end workflow from artwork request to production sign-off, with typical timing at each stage.

### Step 1 — Artwork Request (Day 0)

Triggered immediately after the order is placed. The rep emails the client with the subject line:

**"Order No.: EU#XXXXX - Artwork Needed to Begin Your Order"**

The email asks the client to send artwork in any of the accepted file formats (see Section 2). The rep also asks for: delivery address confirmation, required delivery date (if not already known), and any design preferences or colour codes.

*Typical turnaround: same business day as order*

### Step 2 — Artwork Receipt and Acknowledgement (Day 0–1)

When the client sends artwork, the rep replies immediately to confirm receipt and set expectations. They also acknowledge any specific instructions (dimensions, colour codes, multiple designs, item breakdowns).

The rep then passes the files to the design team for review and mockup creation.

*Standard response: within a few hours of receiving files*

### Step 3 — Design Team Review (Day 1–2)

The design team checks the artwork for:
- File quality and resolution
- Correct dimensions for the product ordered
- Clarity, blurriness, or image artefacts
- Colour accuracy

If issues are found, the team flags them to the rep before sending the mockup. If the issues are fixable (low resolution, colour adjustment needed), they attempt an enhancement. If they are not fixable (lines or artefacts embedded in the source image), the rep contacts the client.

*Internal design turnaround: typically 1 business day*

### Step 4 — Mockup Delivery (Day 1–3)

The rep sends the client the 2D mockup by email with the subject line:

**"Order No.: EU#XXXXX - Your 2D Mockup is Ready – Please Review and Approve"**

For simple products (single wall, single banner), the mockup is typically attached as a JPG. For complex orders (exhibition booths with multiple panels, archwaves, counters, table covers), each surface has its own separate mockup file, delivered as numbered JPGs via a Google Drive link.

The email uses a consistent standard template (see Section 3, Template 2).

*Mockup delivery: Day 1–3 from artwork receipt*

### Step 5 — Client Review and Approval or Revision (Day 2–5)

The client either approves or requests changes. Most threads close at the first or second mockup.

If the client approves: the rep confirms, moves the order to production, and requests a Google/Trustpilot review.

If the client requests changes: the rep acknowledges, asks for any additional information needed (e.g. colour codes, updated files), and sends a revised mockup, usually the same day or next business day.

*Client response window: usually 1–3 days*

### Step 6 — Approval Confirmation and Production Lock-In (Day 3–6)

Once the client approves, the rep sends the approval confirmation email. This email is important: it confirms the order has moved to production, explicitly notes that no further changes or cancellations are possible, provides a tracking update promise, and requests a review.

*After approval: production begins immediately*

### Step 7 — Dispatch and Tracking (Day 8–13 from order)

Once produced and dispatched, the rep shares the DHL tracking number. Standard UK delivery is 5–7 working days from mockup approval. Express shipping (at additional cost) is available and is paid via a separate payment link sent by the rep.

**Typical end-to-end timeline from order to delivery: 8–13 working days (standard) or 5–7 working days (express)**

## Section 2: Artwork File Requirements

### Accepted File Formats

The full list communicated to clients:

**.eps, .png, .jpg, .bmp, .gif, .psd, .cdr, .pdf, .tif, .tiff, .ppt, .ai**

In practice, the formats most commonly mentioned in outgoing emails are **PDF, AI, JPEG, and PNG**. These are the primary formats to request. If a client sends a Canva-exported PDF, it is accepted but must be reviewed by the design team first to confirm resolution before committing to production.

### Resolution

**Minimum 300 DPI** for print. When clients send low-resolution artwork, the design team attempts enhancement but cannot always resolve the issue. Reps should flag this requirement upfront.

### Colour Format

**CMYK is the print standard.** When a client requests a specific colour, ask for the CMYK value (e.g. C 60, M 0, Y 32, K 0) as well as the Hex code. Screen colours (RGB/Hex) are accepted for reference but clients must be reminded that CMYK printing may produce slight colour variations from what they see on screen. This disclaimer is always included in the mockup send email.

### Dimensions

Artwork must match the exact dimensions of the product ordered. The design team checks this as part of their review. If dimensions don't match, the design team adjusts or flags the issue.

### File Transfer

- **Small files:** Sent directly in the email thread as an attachment or Google Drive link
- **Large files (multi-item or high-res):** Clients should use **WeTransfer, Dropbox, or Google Drive** and share the link in the email thread

### Multi-Item Orders

For orders with multiple product variants (e.g. different fabric designs across counters, table covers, and walls), clients should send a clear breakdown in writing — stating which design goes on which item and in what quantity. Reps confirm this breakdown in their reply before passing to the design team.

## Section 4: Common Artwork Problems and How Reps Handle Them

### 1. Wrong Artwork Submitted

**What happens:** The client sends artwork that doesn't match what was discussed or ordered — different logo, wrong campaign, or an earlier version of the design.

**How reps handle it:** Apologise briefly for any confusion, confirm what the correct artwork should be based on the original brief, and commit to reworking the proof immediately.

*Example (Aaron, Email 37):* "I apologise for the oversight regarding the artwork. We will rework the proofs using the WOT and Bushmills artwork you provided in your original email, following the format you specified."

---

### 2. Blurry or Low-Resolution Artwork

**What happens:** The client sends artwork that is too low in resolution to print cleanly.

**How reps handle it:** The design team attempts an enhancement. A proof and a print preview screenshot are sent to the client to show the enhanced result. If enhancement is insufficient, the client is asked to provide a higher-resolution file.

*Example (Simon Diplexcito thread):* "Your artwork is a little bit blurry. So we have enhanced your artwork. We have attached the proof and print preview screenshot below."

---

### 3. Artefacts or Lines Embedded in the Source Image

**What happens:** The mockup shows grid lines, grey banding, or other artefacts that are part of the client's original file rather than a printing error.

**How reps handle it:** Be honest and direct — explain that the issue originates in the source image and cannot be removed on BDS's side. Offer an alternative, such as removing the problematic image element entirely.

*Example (Gladina, Email 64 — SportsPro Media):* "The grey lines are present in the source image itself, and we are unable to remove them on our end. To ensure the best result, would you like us to provide a version of the mockup where the image is removed entirely?"

---

### 4. Canva-Exported Files

**What happens:** Client creates their design in Canva and exports a PDF. Quality varies.

**How reps handle it:** Accept the file, but flag it to the design team first to check resolution and format before confirming. If it passes, proceed to mockup. If it doesn't, ask the client for a higher-quality export.

*Example (Kaviya, Email 95):* "I have received the PDF you created in Canva. I will share this with our design team to confirm if the format and resolution are satisfactory for production. Once they have reviewed the file, we will get back to you with a 2D mockup for your approval or let you know if any adjustments are needed."

---

### 5. Colour Mismatch (Design Doesn't Match Brand Colours)

**What happens:** The initial mockup uses the colours from the file as-provided, but the client says the colours don't match their brand.

**How reps handle it:** Ask for the exact CMYK code (and/or Hex reference), acknowledge the deadline, and promise an updated proof.

*Example (Kaviya, Email 92):* "I have received the updated artwork and shipping label. I will work on adjusting the design to match your brand colors (C 60, M 0, Y 32, K 0 / Hex: #46d2c4) and will send you an updated proof at the earliest."

---

### 6. Mockup Appears Low Quality (Client Concerned About Print Output)

**What happens:** Client receives the mockup and worries that the blurry or low-resolution appearance of the JPG preview means the final print will look the same.

**How reps handle it:** Reassure the client that the mockup is a low-resolution preview only, and the actual print file is full quality.

*Example (Kaviya, Email 63):* "The actual print file is of good quality to print, this is just a low resolution mockup."

---

### 7. Complex Multi-Item Orders (Multiple Designs Across Multiple Products)

**What happens:** The client orders several items (e.g. walls, counters, and table covers) and needs different designs on different pieces.

**How reps handle it:** Ask the client to send a written breakdown listing which design goes on which item and in which quantity, alongside all the corresponding artwork files. Each item gets its own named mockup file (e.g. `EU#17749_S-Shaped Wave_Mockup.jpg`, `EU#17749_Table Cover A x3_Mockup.jpg`). All are shared via a single Google Drive folder link.

*Example (Maisie/Chuckling Cheese, Email 73):* The client sent a detailed breakdown — "S-Shaped Wave Tension Media Wall x 1 - 1 Design Attached / Rectangle Fabric Display Counter x 3 - 2 x Counter A, 1 x Counter B / Fitted Table Cover x 6 — 4 different designs" — which the team used to produce and label each mockup individually.

## Section 5: How BDS Reps Handle Revision Requests

### Tone

Revision responses are always positive and non-defensive. The best reps — particularly Kaviya — treat every revision as a normal, expected part of the process. They never imply the client has made a mistake. Phrases like "No problem at all" and "Happy to make those changes" set the right tone. The reply is always short: acknowledge the change, ask for any additional information needed, and commit to a timeline.

### Process

1. **Read the revision carefully.** Confirm what is changing in your reply so the client knows you've understood correctly.
2. **Ask for what you need.** If the client says "change the background to orange/gold," ask for the exact colour code before the design team begins, to avoid a second revision.
3. **Pass to design team immediately.** Use the phrase "I'll ask our design team to..." or "Our design team will update this and send you a revised mockup shortly."
4. **Set a timing expectation.** "Shortly" or "as soon as possible" is fine for minor changes. For complex revisions, "by end of day" or "tomorrow" is cleaner.

### Limits on Revisions

The email threads do not show a formal limit on the number of revisions. However, the approval confirmation makes clear that once the client approves, no further changes can be made under any circumstances. This is the key boundary: unlimited revisions before approval, zero revisions after.

Practically, deals that require more than two or three rounds of revisions are rare. When a client is making many changes, the rep ensures they gather all remaining changes in one go before sending the final revised mockup for approval.

### After Approval: No Exceptions

Once the approval confirmation has been sent, the order is in the production queue. Reps are firm on this — there is no mechanism to reverse it. If a client tries to request a change after approving, the rep would refer back to the approved mockup and the production lock-in notice.

### Proactive Quality Checks

BDS design team members flag their own concerns to the rep before sending a mockup to the client — for example, flagging a blending issue or a vector image that looks unusual in the preview. This is a quality checkpoint that saves a round of revision.

*Example (Tara, Email 45):* "please could you check the Brain vector image looks blending issue in the flare effect, I attached the screenshot too" — flagged internally before sending to client.
