# BDS UK Sales — Complaints & Issue Resolution Playbook

**Based on:** Analysis of 150 real email threads covering delivery failures, wrong items, damaged goods, print errors, refund disputes, and quality complaints  
**Reps featured:** Kaviya (Sales Manager), Aaron, Tara, Gladina, Jenny, Manusha, Arfath, Ross  
**Last updated:** April 2026

## Section 1: The 8 Most Common Complaint Types and Frequency

### Complaint 1: Missing Items in the Delivery

**Frequency:** The most common complaint type — appears in 8+ threads.

Items arrive but the order is incomplete. The missing components are typically accessories, not the main product. The most commonly missing items across threads are: water bags for flag bases, LED connecting cables, frame connectors, torx screws, bar attachments, blackout rear screens, and carry bags.

**Representative threads:** Emails 8 (Pete Roberts, 3 undelivered packages from a multi-item order), 19 and 24 (Powderkeg Production — 16 × 90 degree bar attachments, 16 × frame connectors, 128 torx screws, 4 × LED cables, 2 spare LED bars, rear blackout screen all missing), 35 (Ghost Lifestyle — LED strips, cables, and screws missing from backdrops per the instruction manual), 55 and 118 (multiple packages still in transit for a Spain-bound order), 67 (Canon — podium accessories not located post-event), 75 (eBlockchain — one roll-up missing from a multi-unit order), 108 (Coaches Congress — water bags for flag stands missing with event starting that evening).

### Complaint 2: Wrong Item Delivered

**Frequency:** 7+ threads.

The client receives an item that doesn't match what was ordered — a different print version, the wrong frame part, the wrong size, or an incorrect configuration. This is distinct from a production error (the product was manufactured correctly, but the wrong one was sent).

**Representative threads:** Emails 28 and 78 (Purple Accountants — corner piece between poles 3 and 4 ordered, poles dispatched instead; then incorrect part sent again on replacement), 60 (Four Quarters Polo — logo version backdrop delivered instead of plain black as repeatedly confirmed via email), 100 (Identity Global — wrong print version sent; only the missing print needed), 120 (Gustaf — order configuration queried), 122 (Lyndsay Wind / Tunnel project — entrance and exit panels were flipped, printed on the wrong sides), 124 (Bamboo Creations — single-sided quoted, double-sided had been agreed).

### Complaint 3: Delivery Failure / Item Never Arrived / Late Arrival

**Frequency:** 7+ threads.

Either the item was never delivered, or it arrived after the client's event date — making it useless. Some cases involve a carrier failing to deliver (UPS, DHL); others are BDS production or dispatch timing failures.

**Representative threads:** Email 1 (UPS failed delivery — Kaviya raised a claim and offered a full refund), Email 13 (Two Pints Deep charity event — air gate not delivered on the day, event went ahead without it, compensation requested), Email 36 (Pierre/ZavePower — order couldn't be collected from the pickup point, missed a trade fair entirely, full refund requested), Email 56 (Surrey Party Hire — DHL notification showed delivery after the client's event date), Email 97 (Arley Medical Services — rep confirmed a delivery date that missed the event by two days; client cancelled order and requested refund), Email 136 (Coaches Congress — water bag replacement offered at 4-5 working days, event started that evening).

### Complaint 4: Print / Production Error

**Frequency:** 6+ threads.

The product arrived and matches the frame, but the print itself contains an error — wrong text, missing design elements, incorrect orientation, or a version that doesn't match the approved mockup.

**Representative threads:** Emails 37 (Stage Engage — stars missing from the front face of the print), Emails 41 and 85 (Oluwatoyin Racheal — significant error on banner text; rep apologised immediately and arranged reprint of the 6m × 2.3m side panel), Email 52 (Multimedia Plus — zips on the 12m media wall didn't match the uprights; reprinted but client asked how they could trust the replacement), Email 16 (Rhotic Media — two mockups incorrect: one missing "2026" from logo, one distorted; screenshots were correct and were approved against), Email 100 (Identity Global — wrong print sent, correct image confirmed in email).

### Complaint 5: Item Damaged in Transit

**Frequency:** 5+ threads.

The product arrives physically damaged — broken connectors, shattered casing, damaged screens, dented or bent frames. Transit damage is the most common cause, though some damage appears to be pre-shipment.

**Representative threads:** Email 47 (Arran Graphics — one small connector shattered in transit; box appeared undamaged externally; extra screen included which caused confusion about installation), Emails 11 and 24 (Powderkeg Production — blackout screens damaged, faulty and exposed wiring, frames described as not safe or fit for purpose on a £5,799 order; partial refund of £2,000 was offered and rejected), Email 64 (Arran Graphics — connector damaged on delivery, rep asked for images to raise a UPS claim and simultaneously dispatched a replacement), Email 80 (Convoy of Hope — top of an item damaged on arrival, replacement requested).

### Complaint 6: Product Doesn't Match Website / Expectations

**Frequency:** 4+ threads.

The item arrived in working order and no physical damage, but the client is disappointed because the product looks different from the website photography or didn't meet their quality expectations.

**Representative threads:** Emails 69 and 87 and 106 (Courtenay Thomas / Delicious Agency — product finish doesn't look like website images; rep responded that no damage = no return for customised items, but the client continued asking for partial compensation), Email 63 and 121 (Alison Maciver — backdrop looks like it was made using AI, no roof tiles visible, "does not look anything like the image I approved"; rep arranged return and initiated refund), Email 2 (air gate print complaint — offered refund on print cost or free replacement, noting frame is a long-term asset).

### Complaint 7: Refund Promised but Not Issued / Delayed

**Frequency:** 4+ threads.

The rep has already acknowledged the complaint and agreed a refund, but the money has not reached the client — sometimes weeks later. This escalates the complaint from a product issue to a trust issue.

**Representative threads:** Email 59 (David Leydon — refund agreed, acknowledged as BDS's fault, but still not issued; client threatening to initiate bank charge-back), Emails 137 and 4 (Duralock — bank details provided for refund, waiting for confirmation), Email 125 (James Brinton — event now over, replacement no longer needed, requesting refund instead), Email 139 (Signs and Designs — waiting for confirmation that refund has been made to account).

### Complaint 8: Faulty Parts / Incompatible Components

**Frequency:** 5+ threads.

A specific component doesn't work correctly or is incompatible with the client's setup — wrong power lead for the UK, incorrect plug type, broken carry case, worn feet, or a part that needs replacing after regular use.

**Representative threads:** Emails 115 and 130 (Esther Holt / Care for the Family — item shipped with an incompatible power lead; client noted adaptors are available but they shouldn't need to buy one; rep dispatched correct UK lead), Email 39 (Owen Jones / First Option Software — needs replacement feet and bolts for a 6m tension fabric backdrop after use), Email 23 (Joe Chambers — carry case for a 2m × 2m pop-up display failed within a year; rep had not responded via WhatsApp despite promising to), Emails 11 and 24 (Powderkeg — faulty wiring in LED light box system described as a safety hazard), Email 26 (Tara to Della Calder / Risktec — items don't come with feet by default, described as an instruction error).

## Section 2: The Step-by-Step Process When a Complaint Comes In

Based on how the best reps handle complaints across the 150 threads, the process runs as follows:

**Step 1: Acknowledge immediately — same day, no exceptions**

The first reply must acknowledge the complaint and apologise without qualification. Reps do not question the client's claim, ask for proof before apologising, or push the issue back to the client. The tone from Kaviya and Gladina is consistent: "I'm really sorry to hear this — that's not the experience we want you to have."

Even when the issue is unclear (the client described something vague), reps respond the same day with an acknowledgement, then follow up with questions.

**Step 2: Ask for what you need to move forward**

Immediately after the apology, ask for the one piece of information needed to resolve the complaint:
- For damaged items: "Please share a photo so I can raise this with our production team / raise a claim with UPS."
- For missing items: "Can you confirm which items are missing and share your delivery address?"
- For wrong items: "Please send a photo of what you received and I'll get the correct item dispatched."
- For refunds: "Could you share your bank details (sort code and account number) so I can initiate the refund?"

Ask for only one thing. Do not send a list of questions that makes the client feel they're being investigated.

**Step 3: Internally verify and raise**

Check the order details, confirm the complaint is accurate against the invoice, and pass to the production or fulfilment team. Kaviya's standard phrasing: "I am looking into this immediately" and "I will update you shortly on the timeline."

If transit damage is suspected, raise a carrier claim (UPS or DHL) in parallel with dispatching the replacement — don't make the client wait for the claim outcome.

**Step 4: Confirm the resolution path and set a timeline**

Before closing the reply, tell the client exactly what is going to happen and when. Reps confirm:
- What will be sent / what refund amount
- When the replacement will be dispatched or when the refund will be initiated
- Delivery timeline for replacements (same 5-7 working days)

Gladina (Email 38): "The replacement print will be delivered by the 18th next week. The items are ready and will be picked up today or tomorrow."

**Step 5: Escalate for high-value disputes or safety issues**

When a complaint involves a significant amount (£1,000+), a safety concern (Powderkeg's faulty wiring), or a client threatening legal action or bank charge-backs (David Leydon), Kaviya escalates. The standard pattern is to offer a partial refund first (with a full replacement as the alternative), then escalate if the client rejects it.

For the Powderkeg case (£5,799 order, multiple failures): Kaviya offered £2,000 partial refund. The client rejected it. The case was escalated and a full refund was under consideration.

**Step 6: Close the complaint explicitly**

Once the resolution has been delivered, send a confirmation email. This prevents clients from following up unnecessarily and ensures there's a clear record of the closure.

## Section 4: Tone and Language That Works — How to De-escalate

### The Core Principle

The fastest way to stop a complaint from escalating is to make the client feel heard within the first reply. Reps who acknowledge the impact of the problem — not just the problem itself — de-escalate fastest.

**Wrong:** "Thank you for your email. Please send photos of the damage so we can investigate."

**Right:** "I'm really sorry to hear this — I can imagine how stressful it is to have this happen before your event. Could you send over a photo? I'm looking into it right now."

The second version acknowledges the impact, skips the corporate "we will investigate" language, and signals immediate action.

### Language Patterns That Work

**"I'm really sorry to hear this"** — Used by Kaviya and Gladina consistently as the opening line for complaint responses. Not "I apologise for any inconvenience" — that phrasing is passive and clients read it as deflection.

**"I'm looking into this immediately"** — Signals urgency without making a promise that might be broken.

**"I'll arrange for the replacement right away"** — Commits to action. Clients who receive this response rarely follow up asking where it is, because they trust it's in motion.

**"Let me know which option works better for you"** — Giving the client a choice (reprint vs. refund) returns a small amount of control to them in a situation where they felt powerless.

**Avoiding bureaucratic language:** Reps don't say "As per our policy" or "We will investigate and revert." These phrases raise the client's defenses. The threads show that clients who receive those responses escalate their tone in the next message.

### Handling Angry or Escalated Clients

When a client is very angry (Powderkeg, David Leydon, Pierre/ZavePower), the rep's job in the first response is not to justify or defend — it's to reduce the temperature.

**What works:** Acknowledge the frustration explicitly. "I completely understand why you're upset — this isn't the standard we hold ourselves to and I'm going to make sure this is resolved." Then propose a concrete next step.

**What doesn't work:** Explaining what went wrong before offering a fix. Clients in active distress are not interested in the explanation yet — they want to know what you're going to do.

**When a client threatens legal action or a bank charge-back:** Don't respond defensively. Acknowledge the frustration, restate the commitment to refund, and ask for bank details to initiate it. The goal is to get ahead of the charge-back, not to argue.

### Aaron's Responses — A Cautionary Note

Several of Aaron's complaint responses in the threads are too brief to be effective. "I apologise. We will have it sent in sometime now." (Email 40) and "You're Welcome." (Email 51) as a response to a multi-part missing items complaint are noted patterns to avoid. Brief responses to complex complaints signal to the client that their problem isn't being taken seriously, and they follow up more aggressively.

A complaint response should never be shorter than 3-4 sentences, even when the fix is simple.

## Section 5: What Causes Complaints — Patterns New Reps Should Avoid

### Pattern 1: Confirming a Delivery Date Before Checking Production

The clearest cause of the most damaging complaints — where the client had an event and the item didn't arrive — is a rep confirming a delivery date without verifying it against the production schedule.

In Email 97, a rep told a client their order could arrive before a 25/26th event. The actual delivery would have been the 27th. The client cancelled the order, demanded a refund, and described being "incredibly disappointed" in the company's responses.

**The fix:** Never confirm a specific delivery date in writing until production has confirmed the slot. The formula is: "Delivery takes 5–7 working days from artwork approval. If we receive approval by [DATE], you'd receive it by [DATE]." Let the client calculate — don't make the promise yourself.

### Pattern 2: Not Following Up on WhatsApp After Promising To

Multiple clients (Email 23, Joe Chambers) report contacting BDS via WhatsApp, being told someone would follow up, and then receiving no message. This creates a second complaint on top of the original one — now the client is angry about the product AND about being ignored.

**The fix:** If you direct a client to WhatsApp or promise to follow up there, treat it as a binding commitment. Set a reminder. Do not let a WhatsApp promise go unfulfilled.

### Pattern 3: Sending the Wrong Print Version Despite Clear Email Confirmation

In Email 60 (Four Quarters Polo), the client had exchanged numerous emails confirming that a plain black backdrop was required — not the logo version. The logo version was delivered. The client wrote: "I'm really not sure how that has happened after our numerous emails."

This category of complaint — wrong version sent when the correct version was clearly specified — often comes from a disconnect between the rep and the production team at order handoff.

**The fix:** At the point of order, include a clear note in the production brief specifying exactly which version was approved and confirmed. Do not rely on the mockup alone — if the client overrode the mockup in writing, that instruction must be captured explicitly.

### Pattern 4: Missing Accessories Not Caught at Dispatch

The most frequent complaint type — missing water bags, connectors, screws, LED cables — points to a picking and packing gap. These are accessories confirmed in the product instructions but not always checked against the order before shipping.

**The fix:** Flag accessory-dependent products (flag stands, LED light boxes, tension fabric kits) as requiring a checklist before dispatch. When a new rep quotes these products, they should also communicate to the client what's included in the package, so the client can spot a missing item immediately rather than discovering it on-site at an event.

### Pattern 5: Incompatible Parts for International Deliveries

Email 115 (Care for the Family) illustrates a recurring issue: products with UK-only power leads being dispatched without noting that international clients will need to purchase adaptors or receive a different lead.

**The fix:** For any order shipping to an address outside mainland UK, confirm power requirements and plug standards before dispatch. If the product includes electrical components, this is a non-negotiable check.

### Pattern 6: Different Reps Asking the Same Client for the Same Information

In Email 33, Ellie Taylor had already sent photos to one rep and received a response. When she followed up, a second rep asked for the same photos again. She threatened to cancel: "If this isn't resolved ASAP I'll be requesting a refund and I'll go elsewhere."

**The fix:** Before responding to a complaint thread, read the full email history. If photos have already been received, say so: "I can see my colleague received your photos yesterday — I've picked this up and here's what we're doing."

### Pattern 7: Listing Incorrect Specifications on the Website

Email 54 (Becky Clayton) complained that a product listing said "width 25mm/1 inch" but the actual item was different. The client had to pay to replace the item and specifically asked BDS to correct the listing.

Website listing errors are rare, but when they cause an order to go wrong, they create a strong and legitimate complaint — because the client ordered exactly what was advertised. When a client raises a spec discrepancy, pass it to the team responsible for the website and confirm to the client that it's been flagged.
