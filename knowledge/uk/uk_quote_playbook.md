## Section 1: How BDS UK Reps Structure a Quote

### The Core Formula

Every UK quote follows the same basic structure, regardless of who sends it or what the product is:

1. **A brief, friendly opener** — one or two lines acknowledging the enquiry
2. **The invoice link** — the quote is always delivered as a clickable link to `backdropsource.co.uk/invoices/[token]`
3. **What's included** — a short note clarifying what the price covers (print, frame, delivery, or print only)
4. **Delivery timeline** — always stated as "5–7 working days from the date of approval"
5. **A soft close** — "have a look and let me know if you have any questions"

The invoice link is the centrepiece of every quote. Reps do not paste pricing tables into the email body unless the client has asked a direct price question or the conversation is a bulk-pricing discussion. For standard enquiries, the link alone is sufficient.

### What Is Always Included in the Quote Email

- The invoice link
- Confirmation of what is or is not included in the price (print only vs. print + frame + delivery)
- Delivery timeline (5–7 working days standard; express available at extra cost)
- An offer to answer questions or make adjustments

### Optional Elements Reps Add Based on Context

- **Reprint-only pricing** — listed separately when the client may need to order replacement prints later (Kaviya, Email 52, pop-up event kit)
- **Delivery destination clarification** — for international or EU orders, notes on customs, duty-free delivery options, and any additional cost (e.g. £9 extra for Berlin delivery)
- **Discount pre-applied** — when a discount has already been applied, reps note "here is the discounted quote" without always stating the percentage
- **"Click here to place your order"** — used by Aaron and Kaviya to add urgency and guide the client directly to checkout
- **Value proposition sentences** — Ross frequently includes a sentence on why BDS is the right supplier (direct manufacturer, premium dye-sub fabric, fast UK turnaround, wholesale pricing)
- **Call offer** — Ross often adds "If you prefer a quick call, share a good time and I'll ring you"

### Rep Style Differences

**Kaviya** — Very clean and efficient. Sends the link, lists reprint costs when relevant, closes with "Have a look and let me know if you have any questions."

**Aaron** — Uses the "We've added your item to the cart for a quick and easy checkout. Click here to place your order" format. Adds a reassurance sentence about the design team reviewing the files and sending a mockup.

**Ross** — Adds more context. Notes wholesale pricing and direct-manufacturer status, offers a call, and usually ends with a Yes/No question to drive a decision.

**Tara** — Sends the invoice link alongside a product link for reference, and may include product specs (frame dimensions, package weight, contents list).

## Section 4: How Reps Handle Discount and Price-Matching Requests

### The General Approach

Reps never refuse a discount request outright — they acknowledge the budget concern, offer what they can, and keep the deal moving. The goal is to avoid losing the sale to price while also not giving away more than necessary.

### Available Discount Codes and Mechanisms

**Code Z5** — 5% off. Offered proactively to new or hesitant clients. Sometimes described as a "small discount" without naming the code.

**Code FIRST7** — 7% off for first orders. Referenced by Ross (Email 58) when working to bring a client's total closer to their stated £500 budget.

**Pre-applied discount** — Reps frequently apply a discount directly at the quote level and send the link as a "discounted quote" without stating the percentage. This keeps the conversation clean while still giving the client a better price.

**Bulk pricing** — At quantity thresholds, per-unit pricing drops automatically. This is the most significant discount lever. At 12 units, per-unit price on a single-sided backdrop with frame drops from £247 to £149 — a 40% reduction. At 72–100 units, further reductions apply.

**Charity discount** — Mentioned explicitly by Ross (Email 64) for a charity client. Applied at quote level.

**Bundle pricing** — When a client is buying multiple product types (e.g. booth kit + banners + table covers), reps offer "bundle savings" or "bundle pricing" to bring the combined total down.

### How Reps Respond When a Client Asks for a Lower Price

**Acknowledge and redirect:** When a client says something is out of budget, the rep first acknowledges the concern, then proposes a configuration change (e.g. single-sided instead of double, no lights, smaller size) that brings the price into range.

*Example (Ross, Email 64 — charity client):* "We can keep costs down with single-sided print and standard frame... I can update your quote with our charity discount and confirm the most cost-effective configuration."

**Negotiate down to available:** When a client requests a specific percentage off that exceeds BDS's limits, reps decline cleanly and offer what they can.

*Example (Email 97 — 20% request):* "While a flat 20% isn't possible, we can sharpen bulk rates once we have your final item list/quantities."

*Example (Lucus, Email 99):* "I spoke with our team; they have mentioned the order above [£]900 can be applicable for a 15% discount. We have provided a 10% discount; this is the best we can offer for this order."

**Threshold framing:** When a client's order is close to a threshold that unlocks a better rate, reps mention the threshold as a way to encourage adding quantity.

**Lock specs first:** Ross often defers the exact discount until the client confirms their product spec — "I can also help with a discount once specs are locked." This prevents discounting a quote that may change.

### What Reps Do NOT Do

- Give blanket percentage discounts without checking with the team
- Offer discounts immediately without acknowledging value first
- Match a competitor's price without context or question
- Make discount promises before the order spec is confirmed

## Section 5: Typical Timeline from Quote to Order

### What the Email Threads Show

The gap between sending a quote and receiving a confirmed order varies widely. Based on the follow-up patterns visible in the threads, the typical UK client decision timeline breaks down roughly as follows:

**Fast decisions (same day to 2 days):** These are clients with an imminent event, returning customers, or clients who reached out already knowing what they wanted. They review the quote quickly and either approve or ask one clarifying question before ordering.

**Standard decisions (3–7 days):** The majority of clients. The rep sends the quote, follows up once at 2–3 days, often receives a question or a revision request, and closes within the week.

**Slow decisions (1–3 weeks):** Common for larger orders, multi-product kits, or clients who need internal sign-off. Ross follows up at 2 days, again at 4–5 days, and again at 1 week with increasing urgency cues tied to event deadlines.

**Dormant (3+ weeks):** A portion of quotes go cold. The rep sends a final long-chase email, often more than 3 weeks after the initial quote, with a clear yes/no ask.

### Ross's Follow-Up Cadence

Ross's follow-up sequence across the threads is the most consistent and best-documented:

- **Day 0:** Quote sent
- **Day 2–3:** First follow-up ("we last heard from you about two days ago…")
- **Day 4–6:** Second follow-up, often with additional value content (product specs, mockup offer, discount mention)
- **Day 7–10:** Third follow-up, stronger deadline urgency tied to the client's event date
- **Day 20+:** Final long-chase, soft close with yes/no, acknowledges they may have moved on

### Urgency Triggers Reps Use to Accelerate the Decision

All reps use deadline-based urgency as the primary close tool:

- Tying the approval deadline to the client's event date: *"To hit your event on [DATE], we'd need approval by [DATE]. Production takes 5–7 working days."*
- Production slot language: *"Moving forward now helps us lock in your production slot and ensure on-time delivery."*
- Express shipping offer: *"If you need it sooner, we can arrange express shipping at an additional cost."*

Reps do not use false scarcity or inventory pressure — the urgency always comes from the client's real event or delivery date.
