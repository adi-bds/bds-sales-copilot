# BDS UK Sales — Follow-Up, Post-Delivery & Reorder Playbook

**Based on:** Analysis of 248 real email threads across three categories: follow-up on pending quotes (88 emails), post-delivery check-ins (682 emails), and returning client reorders (80 emails)  
**Reps featured:** Ross (Manager), Kaviya (Sales Manager), Aaron, Gladina, Jenny, Tara, Arfath  
**Last updated:** April 2026

## Section 1: The Follow-Up Cadence — When Reps Follow Up and What They Say

### The Standard Timing Pattern

Based on the email threads, UK reps follow a consistent cadence after a quote is sent or a conversation goes quiet:

**Day 0:** Quote sent or initial conversation completed.

**Day 2–3:** First follow-up. This is the most common interval across the threads. Ross uses this timing consistently for warm leads: "It's been about 3 days since your chat — just checking in." Aaron targets the same window for checkout abandonment and unresponded enquiries.

**Day 4–6:** Second follow-up if no reply to the first. Still warm in tone, but urgency is raised slightly — usually by tying the approval deadline to the client's event date or production slot.

**Day 7–10:** Third follow-up. Tone becomes more outcome-focused. The message usually includes a clear yes/no question and mentions that production time is now becoming a constraint. This is the point where Ross makes the urgency explicit: "Moving forward this week helps us keep production and delivery right on schedule."

**Day 14+:** Final chase. Used for leads that have gone quiet for over two weeks. Tone is soft and non-pushy — acknowledges they may have moved on, leaves the door open.

### What Each Follow-Up Message Does

Every follow-up in the threads serves one of three functions:

**Function 1 — Recap and restate.** The rep quickly restates what they were discussing, what the client wanted, and what BDS proposed. This gets the client back into the conversation without them having to scroll back through their inbox.

*Ross (Email 24, to Carl):* "Thanks again for your note yesterday — glad to hear you're set on the stretch tablecloth, feather flag, and one S-Wave backdrop. Just wanted to circle back on the backdrop size..."

**Function 2 — Add value.** The follow-up includes something new — a spec detail, mounting option, or compatibility question — that gives the client a reason to re-engage beyond just "did you make a decision?"

*Ross (Email 21, to Luke on fabric fixings):* "Just checking in — I wanted to share 5 ways fabric banners are typically mounted, so you can pick the right option before we quote: pole pockets, grommets, velcro, zip frame, or hanging loops..."

**Function 3 — Create a decision point.** A direct yes/no close that makes it easy to say yes. This is the most-used technique by Ross and appears in approximately 70% of his follow-up emails. It removes friction — the client doesn't have to write a considered reply, they just have to say yes.

*Ross (Email 7, to Mitchell on LED lightbox):* "Would you like me to lock in a formal quote for the 4m x 2.5m double-sided lightbox now? Yes/No"

### How Ross Structures a Follow-Up (The Gold Standard)

Ross's follow-up emails consistently follow this structure:

1. **Friendly opener with time reference** — "It's been about 3–4 days since our last message — just checking in..."
2. **One-line recap** of what the client said they needed
3. **A small value-add** — a detail, option, or question that shows you've been thinking about their problem
4. **An urgency note** — typically tied to their event date or the 5–7 working day production window
5. **A discount mention** if relevant — Code Z5 (5% off) is offered proactively to new/warm leads who haven't committed
6. **A one-line Yes/No close** — specific to what they would like you to do next

### Kaviya's Follow-Up Style

Kaviya uses a more direct, administrative approach for pending orders — typically for clients who have a quote in hand but haven't approved artwork or confirmed payment. Her tone is brief: "Any update on this? We need to close all outstandings this month." This is different from Ross's warmer approach — it works on clients who have already committed but are dragging their feet, not on leads who are still evaluating.

### Aaron's Follow-Up Style

Aaron uses follow-up emails most effectively for cart abandonment and unconverted web enquiries. His opening is: "We noticed you recently explored some of our products and began the checkout process — we just wanted to reach out to make sure you had all the information you needed." The tone is warm and non-aggressive; it offers help rather than pressure.

### Urgency Triggers Used in Follow-Ups

Every follow-up in the threads connects urgency to something real — reps never use artificial scarcity. The urgency sources used, in order of frequency:

**Event dates:** The most powerful trigger. Tying approval to a specific delivery deadline. "If you're aiming to be event-ready by [DATE], we'd need to move into production by [DATE]."

**Production slot:** "Moving forward now helps us lock in your production slot." This language is used when the client has a tight window but hasn't named an event date explicitly.

**5–7 working day reminder:** Always stated as a fact, not a threat: "Our standard turnaround is 5–7 working days from artwork approval — just so you can plan."

**Month-end close:** Kaviya uses this for pending approvals: "We need to close all outstandings this month." Used sparingly and only on already-committed clients.

## Section 2: Three Follow-Up Email Templates

---

### Template 1: 2-Day Follow-Up (First Touch After Quote)

**When to use:** 2–3 days after sending a quote or after an initial enquiry conversation with no response. The tone is warm and helpful — you are checking in, not chasing.

---

Hi [CLIENT_NAME],

Hope you're having a good week — it's been a couple of days since we last spoke, so I wanted to check in and make sure the quote landed okay.

To recap: I put together a quote for [PRODUCT DESCRIPTION] at [PRICE], which includes [print / print + frame / delivery]. Have a look here if you haven't already: [INVOICE_LINK]

If you have any questions about the spec, the timeline, or anything else — happy to answer them now. And if you're ready to go ahead, just let me know and I can get things moving.

Would you like to proceed, or is there anything you'd like adjusted? Yes/No is perfect.

### Template 2: 5-Day Follow-Up (Second Touch — Adding Urgency)

**When to use:** 4–6 days after the quote with no reply to the first follow-up. Now is the time to connect the decision to the client's delivery deadline.

---

Hi [CLIENT_NAME],

Just a quick follow-up — I know things get busy. I wanted to make sure you had everything you need to move forward when you're ready.

One thing worth flagging: our standard production is 5–7 working days from artwork approval. If you have an event or deadline coming up around [DATE], we'd want to get approval confirmed by [APPROVAL DEADLINE DATE] to keep delivery on track.

I can also apply code **Z5** at checkout for 5% off if that helps — just let me know.

Happy to adjust the spec, add a reprint-only option, or send over an updated quote if anything has changed. Otherwise — ready to go when you are.

Shall I keep this quote open as is, or would you like me to make any changes? Yes/No.

[REP_SIGNATURE]

---

### Template 3: 10-Day Follow-Up (Third Touch — Soft Final Chase)

**When to use:** 8–12 days after the quote with no engagement. Tone stays warm but the message is outcome-focused — you are giving the client a clean decision point and not leaving them in limbo.

---

Hi [CLIENT_NAME],

I'm just following up one more time on the quote for [PRODUCT] I sent over [X] days ago.

I completely understand if priorities have shifted — but I wanted to reach out before closing this off, in case you'd still like to go ahead or would prefer an updated quote.

If you're still planning this, I'd suggest moving forward soon — production takes 5–7 working days from artwork approval, and I'd hate for you to miss your window.

If you've decided to go a different direction, a quick note either way would be much appreciated — it helps me keep everything organised on our end.

Ready to proceed? Would you like me to keep the quote open or update anything? Yes/No.

[REP_SIGNATURE]

## Section 3: How Reps Approach Post-Delivery Check-Ins

### Timing

Based on the threads, post-delivery check-ins are sent within 1–3 days of confirmed delivery — sometimes the same day the tracking updates to "delivered." Reps do not wait for the client to reach out first.

### What the Post-Delivery Email Is For

The post-delivery check-in in the BDS threads serves two distinct purposes, and understanding which one applies changes the tone:

**Purpose A — Relationship maintenance (happy client expected):** For straightforward orders where nothing went wrong. The rep sends a short warm message, thanks the client, and asks for a Google or Trustpilot review. No product push — just goodwill and reputation-building.

*Ross (to Helmut, Email 13):* "We'd greatly appreciate it if you could take a moment to review us on Google and Trustpilot. Your feedback means a great deal to us and helps others in the events industry find us — here's the link: [GOOGLE REVIEW LINK] [TRUSTPILOT LINK]"

**Purpose B — Relationship maintenance plus forward signal:** When the rep knows the client has upcoming events, they check in on the delivery AND plant a seed for the next order. This appears in Ross's threads when he already knows from the initial conversation that the client runs regular events or has multiple dates planned.

*Ross (to Helmut):* "We're also excited to hear you're considering more orders for your event in February. Just to confirm — yes, we do deliver to Kenya." The reorder prompt is embedded naturally into the delivery check-in.

### Tone and Language for Post-Delivery Check-Ins

The tone across all reps is consistently grateful and brief. These are short emails — usually 3-5 sentences. Reps don't over-explain. They thank the client, ask for the review, and leave the door open.

What works:
- Acknowledging the specific order ("your 2m × 2.3m backdrop for the [event]") rather than using generic language
- Asking for the review with a direct link, not just mentioning it
- Offering to help with "anything for next time" — keeping the relationship open without being pushy

What doesn't appear in the threads:
- Long post-delivery questionnaires or satisfaction surveys
- Pushing a new product immediately after delivery
- Asking for a review before confirming the item arrived as expected

### What Reps Ask in Post-Delivery Emails

In order of frequency across the threads:

**Google review** — Linked directly. The most consistent ask across all reps.

**Trustpilot review** — Usually paired with Google, not asked for separately.

**Video testimonial** — Asked once (Ross to Helmut, a client who had expressed strong satisfaction and agreed to record one). Not a standard ask.

**"How did everything arrive?"** — Sometimes included as a soft opener before the review ask. If the client mentions an issue, the email pivots immediately to a resolution rather than continuing to push for the review.

**Reorder intention signal** — Planted as a question when the rep already knows about upcoming events: "Do you have any upcoming events we can help with?"

### The Post-Delivery Check-In Formula

1. **One warm opener** acknowledging the delivery: "I hope everything arrived in great shape for your event."
2. **Review ask with direct links** to Google and Trustpilot.
3. **Optional:** A sentence about future events or a reorder door-opener.
4. **Signature** — no long sales copy, no product information.

## Section 4: How Reps Handle Reorder Conversations

### What Triggers a Reorder

Based on the threads, returning clients come back for one of four reasons:

**Exact reorder (same product, same spec):** The client needs exactly what they had before. Often includes the previous order number as a reference point. These are the easiest conversations — the rep simply confirms the spec hasn't changed and sends a new invoice.

*Antony Walker (Email 25):* "We ordered Standard Table Covers 2.4m back in May this year. Would it be possible to reorder another?"

*Lorrie Evangelista (Email 9):* "I will be placing an order for another SEG Frameless replacement fabric — 1m W × 2m H double-sided print. Same address as before."

**Duplicate / scale-up:** The client loved the product and wants the same thing again, but in higher quantity or for a different location.

*Nicole Lindsay (Email 6):* "Can I please have another wall with 5 shelves exactly the same? This will be for two separate events so I need the same × 2."

**Variant or modification:** The client wants a similar product but with a spec change — different size, different fabric type, different side count, or a modular add-on to extend an existing system.

*Richard Buck (Email 3, 17):* "Another custom cover — but I want one-sided blockout fabric this time, specifically NOT duplex."

*Richard (Email 33):* "We have one of your 3m × 3m banners in our warehouse. Can we buy additional frames (1m × 2) to make it 4m wide, and then a 4m × 3m print?"

**Replacement print:** The frame is fine; the fabric has worn, been updated with new branding, or simply needs refreshing. These are regular recurring revenue and very low-friction.

*Samantha Mostert (Email 28):* "We ordered a few stands a few weeks ago. We're looking at ordering another one stand 3m × 2m — would you be able to ship it with the counter if we place the order today?"

### What Reps Say When a Reorder Comes In

The reorder process is streamlined by default. Reps do not treat returning clients as new enquiries — they skip the product education and move straight to order confirmation.

**Ross's reorder sequence (Email 2, to Jessica):**
1. Acknowledge the intent: "Happy to help and fast-track this for you."
2. Confirm the spec: "To match your previous order, could you confirm: the email used last time, the size and fabric, and your shipping address and deadline?"
3. Offer loyalty value: "As a returning customer, I'll also make sure you get our best price — feel free to use code Z5 for an extra 5% off."
4. Close with a yes/no: "Would you like me to draft the reorder now? Yes/No"

**Jenny's reorder sequence (Email 7 and 15, to returning clients asking for banners):**
When a returning client isn't sure which product model they need, Jenny sends a short menu of options — typically 4 roll-up banner variants (budget, teardrop, silver step, double-screen) with links — and asks the client to pick. She adds a deadline note: "If the order is placed and artwork provided today before end of day, we can deliver by [DATE]."

### Loyalty Language and Returning Customer Incentives

Code Z5 (5% off) is the standard returning-customer incentive. Ross uses it proactively in his reorder replies — he doesn't wait to be asked.

Beyond Z5, the main loyalty signal is speed. Reps tell returning clients they'll "fast-track" the quote, prioritise production, and expedite artwork review. This matters more to most returning clients than a discount — they already know the product works and they just need it quickly.

The phrase "as a returning customer" appears consistently in Ross's reorder emails and functions as a trust signal. It tells the client they're not starting from scratch — their preferences are known and will be respected.

### What Reps Do NOT Do in Reorder Conversations

They do not push new products unprompted during a reorder. If a client comes back for an exact replacement, the rep fulfils that request efficiently. Cross-selling happens in post-delivery check-ins or in follow-up conversations after the reorder is confirmed — not as a barrier to placing the reorder.

They do not make the client re-explain their original order from scratch. Reps ask for the previous order number (EU#) or the email address used, then look it up. This is framed as a convenience: "I'll pull up your previous order to make sure we match everything exactly."

## Section 5: How to Spot a Client Likely to Reorder — and Reach Out First

### The Signals in the Email Threads

Several patterns predict a reorder before the client contacts BDS. Reps who spot these signals and reach out first convert at a much higher rate than those who wait.

**Signal 1: The client mentioned a future event in their original order conversation**

This is the clearest reorder signal. When a client says "we have another event in March" or "we do this every quarter," that's a specific reorder trigger. The rep should make a note of that date and reach out 3–4 weeks before it.

In the threads, Ross does this explicitly. After Helmut's post-delivery call, Ross noted that the client had mentioned a February event and immediately confirmed delivery capability to Kenya. He didn't wait for Helmut to come back — he planted the next conversation at the moment of highest satisfaction.

**Signal 2: The client ordered consumables or print-only products**

Print-only orders (backdrop fabric without a frame, replacement SEG graphics, reprint of a banner) are by definition recurring. The frame is a long-term asset; the print isn't. Clients who ordered print-only in their first order almost always need a new print eventually — either because the branding has changed, the fabric has worn, or the event theme is different.

Reach out to print-only clients every 6–9 months with a simple: "We wanted to check in — it's been about [X] months since your last print. If you're planning any events or need a refresh, we're ready to move quickly."

**Signal 3: The client ordered for an event with a recurring date**

Annual conferences, quarterly trade shows, seasonal campaigns — when the original order context reveals a regularly scheduled event, the reorder is predictable. Set a reminder 4–6 weeks before the next likely date and reach out first.

Lorrie Evangelista (Email 9) ordered a replacement print referencing a delivery deadline of January 26 — suggesting a regular January event. A proactive reach-out the following November ("Are you planning to attend the same event this year?") would capture this before she has to chase BDS.

**Signal 4: The client ordered multiple products in their first order**

Multi-product first orders (booth kit + banners + table covers) indicate a well-resourced client with ongoing event presence. These clients often need reprints, additional units, or complementary products for subsequent events.

Reach out 3–4 months after a multi-product order with a check-in: "How did everything hold up at [event]? If you're planning the next one, we can get things moving quickly — and we have your spec on file."

**Signal 5: The client expressed strong satisfaction at delivery**

Sam Kelly (Email 16): "Just wanted to reach out and say thanks for sending the replacement pieces... I'll be reaching out again in the coming week or so for another couple of table cloths."

Luna Vanderispaillie (Email 43): "We will definitely provide a review on Google and Trustpilot." Then ordered again.

Melanie Horn (Email 59): "Thank you so much — great team!!!!"

Clients who are enthusiastic at delivery are your best reorder candidates. Don't wait for them to come to you. When a client gives an effusive response, reply with warmth and plant the next conversation: "Really glad to hear it — we'll be here whenever you're ready for the next event."

### Proactive Reorder Outreach — What to Say

When you're reaching out proactively — before the client has asked — the tone should feel like a check-in, not a sales call. The goal is to make the client feel thought-about, not targeted.

---

Hi [CLIENT_NAME],

Hope you're well — I just wanted to check in as it's been a few months since your [PRODUCT] order.

If you're planning any upcoming events or thinking about a refresh on your branding, we have your previous spec on file and can get a quote together quickly. We also offer code Z5 for 5% off for returning customers.

Let me know if anything's coming up — happy to help.

[REP_SIGNATURE]

---

**Proactive reorder email (ahead of a known annual event):**

Hi [CLIENT_NAME],

Hope all is well. We worked together last [MONTH/YEAR] on your [PRODUCT] for [EVENT]. If that event is coming around again this year, we'd love to help you get set up again.

We have your previous artwork and spec on file, so getting a reorder together would be fast. Would it be useful to put a quote together now so you're ready well ahead of the date?

| Stage | Timing | Rep Action | Tone |

| Second follow-up | Day 4–6 | Add urgency via event date / production | Slightly more urgent |
| Third follow-up | Day 7–10 | Clear yes/no close, final production warning | Outcome-focused |
| Final chase | Day 14+ | Soft close, leave door open | Non-pushy, honest |
| Post-delivery | 1–3 days after delivery | Thank + review request | Grateful, brief |
| Reorder trigger spotted | 3–6 months post-delivery | Proactive reach-out before client asks | Warm, check-in style |
