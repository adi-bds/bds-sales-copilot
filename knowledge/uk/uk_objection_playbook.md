## Section 1: The 8 Most Common Objections — With Exact Client Language

### Objection 1: "It's too expensive / out of our budget"

The most frequent objection in the threads. Clients rarely say the product is bad — they say it doesn't fit their budget or they can't justify the cost.

**Exact language used by clients:**
- "It's out of my budget"
- "Both options are over their budget"
- "We cannot justify using this for only one year"
- "The price is a bit more than we were hoping to spend"
- "Could you do anything to bring the price down?"
- "Is there any flexibility on the price?"

**Frequency:** Appears in 8+ threads across Emails 26, 45, 58, 96, 100, 107, 118, 134.

### Objection 2: "I can get it cheaper elsewhere / competitor price comparison"

Clients often arrive with a cheaper quote from a competitor and use it as leverage. The most cited competitors are XL Displays and Bizay.co.uk.

**Exact language used by clients:**
- "I got it much cheaper locally"
- "I received a much better price from XL Displays"
- "If your price remains £100 per roll-up I obviously have to order from Bizay.co.uk"
- "We've had a quote from another supplier that's significantly lower"
- "Your competitor is offering the same spec for less"

**Frequency:** Appears in Emails 20, 26, 62, 78, 136.

### Objection 3: "Can I have a bigger / better discount?"

Distinct from a general budget objection — the client acknowledges the product is right but actively negotiates the percentage off. Often anchors high to leave room to settle.

**Exact language used by clients:**
- "Is there a better discount?"
- "I think the only thing putting me off right now is the price — perhaps 15%?"
- "Let's go with 25% discount and I will consider it"
- "I know the markup on these products"
- "Can you do 20% for us?"
- "If you can get to £X I'll place the order today"

**Frequency:** The most frequently appearing objection — Emails 8, 12, 30, 95, 99, 100, 112, 120, 126, 134, 135, 141, 145, 148 (14+ threads).

## Section 2: The Most Effective Response to Each Objection

### Objection 1: "Too expensive / out of budget"

**What works:** Acknowledge the budget constraint immediately, then offer a configuration change — not just a discount. Reps who pivot to a smaller size, single-sided instead of double, or print-only instead of print-plus-frame close more frequently than those who just offer a percentage off.

**The winning pattern:**
1. Acknowledge: "I completely understand — let me see what we can do."
2. Propose an alternative configuration that hits the budget: "If we go single-sided and print-only, I can bring this to £[X]."
3. Send a revised quote link — don't just describe the change, show it.

**When to add a discount:** Only after the configuration change is proposed. If the client is still over budget after the spec adjustment, then apply a code (Z5 or FIRST7) on top.

---

### Objection 2: "Competitor price comparison"

**What works:** Ross's response (Email 62) is the gold standard: *"Share the competitor quote here and we'll match or beat it."* This does two things — it calls the client's bluff (many don't have a real quote) and it signals confidence.

**The winning pattern:**
1. Ask to see the competitor quote: "Could you share the details of what they've quoted? I want to make sure we're comparing like for like."
2. If a real quote is presented, match or beat it where possible.
3. If the competitor quote is for a lower-spec product, explain the difference: "Our fabric is 260gsm dye-sublimation — if theirs is a different material, that's why the price is lower."
4. If buying in bulk, offer to beat the price outright: "At the quantities you're looking at, we can come in below that."

**What to avoid:** Never dismiss the competitor without acknowledging the client's position. And never promise to match before you've seen the actual quote.

---

### Objection 3: "Can I have a bigger discount?"

**What works:** Acknowledge the ask, be transparent about limits, and offer what's actually available. Reps who decline cleanly and explain the constraint ("that's above what we're authorised to do without management sign-off") lose fewer deals than those who just say no.

**The winning pattern:**
1. Acknowledge: "I appreciate you being direct — let me tell you what I can do."
2. Name the available discount or threshold: "The best I can apply here is 10%. If the order total goes above £900, that unlocks 15%."
3. If the client pushes further: "A flat 20% isn't something I can authorise on this order size, but if you can confirm the full item list, I can check what we can do on the complete order."
4. Hold on the number — don't cascade upward with each objection. The first offer should be the real offer.

---

### Objection 4: "The timeline won't work"

**What works:** Honesty first. Reps who fabricate a delivery promise they can't meet lose the client permanently. The better approach is to give the accurate timeline, flag express options immediately, and let the client decide.

**The winning pattern:**
1. Confirm the actual lead time clearly: "Our standard is 5–7 working days from artwork approval — that puts delivery on [DATE]."
2. Immediately offer express: "We do offer express production and shipping at an additional cost, which would bring that to [X] days."
3. Give the client a decision point: "If you can approve artwork by [DATE], I can guarantee delivery before your event. Does that work?"
4. If it genuinely can't be done: say so. Don't over-promise. Offering a partial solution (e.g., print only shipped express, frame shipped standard) can sometimes save a partial order.

---

### Objection 5: "Wrong item / missing part"

**What works:** Immediate ownership. The rep does not question the client or ask them to prove the error — they accept the report, ask for the order number, and initiate the fix in the same message.

**The winning pattern:**
1. Apologise without caveats: "I'm really sorry about this — that's not the experience we want you to have."
2. Ask for what you need to resolve it: "Could you send a photo of what arrived so we can raise this with our production team right away?"
3. Confirm the resolution path: "Once I've got the photo, I'll get a replacement organised / arrange collection of the incorrect item."
4. Set a timeline for the fix: "I'll come back to you within [X] hours with confirmation."

---

### Objection 6: "Quality concern"

**What works:** Take the claim seriously, ask for photographic evidence, and escalate internally without making the client feel they need to fight for a resolution. Offering a reprint or credit without waiting for a formal process signals that BDS stands behind the product.

**The winning pattern:**
1. Acknowledge and apologise: "I'm really sorry to hear this — that's not the standard we work to."
2. Request evidence: "Please send over a photo so I can pass this to our quality team immediately."
3. Propose the resolution before the client asks: "Depending on what the photos show, we'll either arrange a reprint or issue a credit — whichever works better for you."
4. If a reprint is not fast enough for the client's deadline: offer a partial refund for the print cost while they source locally.

---

### Objection 7: "Discount code not working"

**What works:** This is a pure service issue, not a negotiation. Reps resolve it fastest by applying the discount manually at the invoice level rather than troubleshooting the code with the client.

**The winning pattern:**
1. Acknowledge immediately: "I'm sorry about that — let me sort it for you right now."
2. Apply the discount manually: "I've updated the quote with the 7% discount applied. Here's your revised invoice: [LINK]."
3. Don't ask the client to retry the code — just fix it on your end.

---

### Objection 8: "Product won't work for our setup / spec mismatch"

**What works:** Ask a clarifying question first to understand the exact constraint, then propose an alternative configuration. Don't assume the client knows all the options — they often don't.

**The winning pattern:**
1. Clarify the constraint: "Could you tell me a bit more about your current setup / venue height / existing frames?"
2. Propose an alternative: "Based on that, I'd suggest [PRODUCT] instead — it uses [METHOD] which should work with what you already have."
3. If BDS genuinely can't help with that spec: say so clearly and offer to refer them. Don't waste time on an order that won't work.

## Section 4: How the Best Reps Handle Competitor Price Comparisons

### The Core Principle

BDS reps never dismiss a competitor quote without first seeing it. The standard approach is to ask for the quote, compare specs, and respond with a specific counter-offer — not a vague "we can do better."

### Ross's Approach (Gold Standard)

Ross (Email 62) handles competitor comparisons with a simple, confident offer: *"Share the competitor quote here and we'll match or beat it."*

This approach works because:
- It signals confidence in BDS's pricing without making a promise before knowing the number
- It forces the client to produce the actual quote — many don't have one, or find it's not like-for-like
- It moves the conversation from an abstract price objection to a concrete comparison BDS can win

When the order is a bulk quantity, Ross goes further: *"If you're buying in bulk, we can beat that price."*

### How Reps Check for Like-for-Like Comparisons

Before matching a competitor price, reps ask about:
- Material spec (is it the same gsm fabric? Same printing method?)
- Frame quality (aluminium vs. cheaper alternatives)
- Delivery included or excluded
- Print-only vs. full kit

BDS's position is quality, not cheapness — so if the competitor is cheaper because the spec is lower, the rep makes that clear rather than racing to the bottom.

### When Reps Don't Match

If the competitor is quoting a genuinely different product (e.g., PVC vs. fabric, different size), reps explain the distinction and hold the BDS price. They don't undercut their own product to win a price war on a different specification.

## Section 5: When Reps Offer Discounts vs. When They Hold Firm

### The Pattern: What Triggers an Offer

Based on the threads, reps move to offer a discount when one or more of these conditions is present:

**Repeat or trade customer:** Kaviya (Email 4) offered 20% to a long-term event client (SportsPro, multiple 2026 events). Trade and agency clients managing multiple events per year get higher rates without being asked.

**Bulk quantity:** The most reliable trigger. At 12+ units, per-unit pricing drops significantly. At 72–100 units, further reductions apply. Reps flag these thresholds proactively: "If you can go to [X] units, I can bring the per-unit cost down to £[Y]."

**Competitor quote presented:** When a real competitor quote is shared, reps match or beat — not as a discount, but as a price adjustment. This is treated differently from a pure negotiation.

**Budget explicitly stated:** When a client says "our total budget is £X," reps work backwards from that number to build a configuration that fits, combining spec adjustments and discounts to get there.

**Charity or non-profit:** Mentioned explicitly by Ross (Email 64). Applied at quote level, no fixed percentage specified.

**Bundle order:** When a client orders multiple product types (backdrop + banners + table cover + booth kit), reps apply bundle pricing rather than individual item pricing.

### The Pattern: When Reps Hold Firm

Reps do not offer discounts — or decline to go further — when:

**The requested percentage exceeds what's authorised:** Lucus (Email 99): *"I spoke with our team; they have mentioned the order above £900 can be applicable for a 15% discount. We have provided a 10% discount; this is the best we can offer for this order."* The percentage ceiling is stated clearly and not moved.

**The spec hasn't been confirmed yet:** Ross often defers a discount until the client locks in the product spec — *"I can also help with a discount once specs are confirmed."* This prevents discounting a quote that will change anyway.

**The client's request is vague or unreasonably high:** When clients ask for 25% or 30% without context, reps acknowledge the ask and offer a realistic alternative rather than negotiating up from zero.

**Delivery timeline is the real issue:** When a client says the price is the problem but the conversation reveals they can't hit the artwork deadline, reps focus on the timeline rather than adding a discount that won't close the deal.

### The Rule Most Reps Follow

The first offer is the real offer. Reps who cascade discounts upward (5% → 10% → 15% in response to repeated pushback) train clients to push harder. The stronger reps name their best offer once, explain the limit, and hold. Ross and Kaviya follow this most consistently — Lucus and Aaron are the clearest examples of stating the ceiling directly and not moving past it.
