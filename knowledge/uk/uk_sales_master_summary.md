# BDS UK Sales — Master Summary

**Based on:** Six individual playbooks synthesized from 1,368 real BDS UK sales emails  
**Covers:** Initial inquiry → Quote → Mockup → Objection handling → Follow-up → Post-delivery → Reorder → Complaints  
**Last updated:** April 2026

## Section 1: How the BDS UK Sales Process Works — From First Contact to Completed Order

This is what actually happens across the 1,368 emails, condensed into the real sequence of events.

### Stage 1 — First Contact (Day 0)

A client reaches out via the website chat, email, or WhatsApp with one of ten common inquiry types. The most frequent is an exhibition or trade show booth inquiry (22%), followed by replacement graphics for an existing frame (16%), and a direct quote request with specific dimensions (14%). A smaller but significant portion ask about shell scheme graphics, custom sizes, on-site installation, or artwork file requirements.

The rep's first reply sets the tone for the entire relationship. The best reps respond the same day, confirm what they understood the client to need, answer the question directly, ask a maximum of three qualifying questions (event date, dimensions, artwork status), add one or two sentences of product credibility, state the delivery timeline, and close with a binary yes/no question. This first response typically includes an offer of code Z5 (5% off) or notes that a discount is available.

### Stage 2 — Quoting (Day 0–1)

Quotes are delivered as a clickable invoice link to `backdropsource.co.uk/invoices/[token]` — never as a table pasted into the email body. The rep notes what's included (print only, print + frame, or print + frame + delivery), confirms the 5–7 working day lead time, and offers to answer questions. For multi-product orders, reprint-only costs are listed separately as a convenience for the client. For bulk orders, tiered pricing is shown. Discounts are typically pre-applied at the invoice level rather than requiring the client to enter a code.

Most UK clients fall into one of four decision speeds: same-day (imminent event or returning customer), 3–7 days (majority), 1–3 weeks (large orders requiring internal sign-off), or dormant (quote goes cold). The follow-up cadence is calibrated to these windows.

### Stage 3 — Mockup and Artwork (Day 0–5)

Once an order is placed (or, for some clients, before payment as a decision aid), the rep requests artwork. Accepted formats include PDF, AI, JPEG, PNG, EPS, and PSD at 300 DPI minimum. The design team creates a 2D mockup — a low-resolution preview sent as a JPG for simple products or via a Google Drive folder for multi-item orders — and the rep sends it with the subject line: *"Order No.: EU#XXXXX - Your 2D Mockup is Ready – Please Review and Approve."*

Revision cycles are typically one or two rounds. Common issues flagged are low-resolution artwork, colour mismatches (always resolved with an exact CMYK code), and artefacts in the source file. Once the client approves, the rep sends an approval confirmation email that locks the order into production — no further changes or cancellations are possible from this point.

### Stage 4 — Objection Handling (Throughout the Sales Cycle)

The three most common objections are: the price is too high (handled by offering a configuration change before a discount), the delivery timeline won't work (handled with honesty about the real date and an express option), and a request for a bigger discount (handled by naming the ceiling once and holding it). Competitor quotes are met with a confident "share it and we'll match or beat it" — not a defensive response.

Discount codes available: Z5 (5%), FIRST7 (7%), with bulk pricing unlocking at 12 units (~40% per-unit reduction) and 15% available on orders over £900 with team approval.

### Stage 5 — Production and Delivery (Day 5–13 from order)

Standard UK production is 5–7 working days from artwork approval. Express options are available at additional cost. Once dispatched, the rep shares a DHL tracking number. The end-to-end timeline from order placement to delivery is typically 8–13 working days for standard and 5–7 for express. International delivery (EU, US) carries additional considerations for customs, EORI numbers, and additional delivery costs.

### Stage 6 — Post-Delivery (Day 1–3 after delivery)

Within 1–3 days of confirmed delivery, the rep sends a brief check-in email. For most orders, this has two goals: confirm everything arrived well and ask for a Google and Trustpilot review with direct links. For clients who mentioned upcoming events, this check-in also plants the next conversation by referencing the future date.

### Stage 7 — Complaint Resolution (When Required)

The most common complaint types are missing items in a delivery, wrong item sent, late or failed delivery, and print/production errors. All are handled with immediate acknowledgement, a photo request where relevant, a clear resolution path (reprint, replacement, or refund), and a confirmed timeline. Refunds are initiated by bank transfer after collecting sort code and account number. Return address: Bpworks INC, C/o DMC Distribution Ltd, Ensor Way, New Mills, High Peak, SK22 4NQ.

### Stage 8 — Reorder (Weeks to Months Later)

Returning clients come back for one of four reasons: exact reorder of the same product, scale-up for additional events, a variant or modification to the original, or a replacement print for an existing frame. Reps fast-track reorders by looking up the previous order number (EU#), confirming the spec, offering code Z5, and closing with "Would you like me to draft the reorder now? Yes/No."

## Section 2: The Top 5 Things the Best UK Reps Do That Others Don't

### 1. They always end with a Yes/No question

Ross does this in approximately 70% of his emails. It is the single most consistent differentiator between his emails and weaker responses in the threads. The Yes/No close removes friction from the client's reply — they don't need to compose a considered email, they just need to say yes. It also forces the rep to have a specific, actionable ask at the end of every message rather than trailing off with "let me know if you have any questions."

Examples from the threads:  
*"Would you like me to lock in a formal quote for the 4m × 2.5m lightbox now? Yes/No"*  
*"Shall we proceed as shown? Yes/No"*  
*"Would you like me to draft the reorder now? Yes/No"*

### 2. They tie urgency to the client's real event date — not to artificial pressure

Every follow-up, every quote, every rush-order conversation in the best threads connects the decision timeline to something the client actually cares about: the date of their event. "To hit your event on [DATE], we'd need artwork approved by [DATE] — production takes 5–7 working days." This is compelling because it's true, and it makes the urgency the client's problem to solve, not a sales tactic.

Weaker reps use vague urgency ("don't wait too long") or no urgency at all. The best reps calculate the date and state it plainly.

### 3. They ask for the competitor quote before agreeing to match it

Ross's response to price comparisons is the most effective in the file: *"Share the competitor quote here and we'll match or beat it."* This response is confident, non-defensive, and strategically sound. Many clients who cite a competitor don't actually have a quote. Those who do often find it's not like-for-like. The act of asking for the quote rather than scrambling to discount transforms a price negotiation into a specification comparison — which BDS consistently wins on material quality.

Lesser reps either become defensive, discount immediately, or dismiss the competitor without engaging. None of these work.

### 4. They propose a configuration change before offering a discount

When a client says the price is too high, the best reps don't jump straight to a percentage off. They propose a change to the product spec — single-sided instead of double, print-only instead of print-plus-frame, smaller size, fewer units — that reduces the cost by design rather than by margin. Only if the client is still over budget after the configuration change does a discount code enter the conversation.

This matters because it trains clients to view the price as tied to the spec rather than to BDS's flexibility. It also means the rep doesn't give away margin unnecessarily on clients who would have bought anyway.

### 5. They plant the next order at the moment of highest client satisfaction

The best post-delivery emails do two things simultaneously: thank the client and open the door to the next conversation. Ross does this most clearly — confirming the delivery, asking for a review, and in the same email noting: "We're also excited to hear you're considering more orders for your February event." The reorder conversation is started at the moment when the client is most satisfied, not weeks later when enthusiasm has faded.

## Section 3: The Top 5 Mistakes That Show Up Repeatedly in Weaker Emails

### 1. Sending a bare invoice link with no context

Multiple threads show a rep sending a single line like "Here's your quote — have a look and let me know" with no explanation of what's included, what the delivery timeline is, or what the next step is. This puts all the cognitive work on the client. A good quote email takes 60 extra seconds to write and significantly reduces the number of clarifying questions that come back.

### 2. Replying too briefly to complex messages

Several reps — Aaron most noticeably in complaint threads — respond to multi-part client messages with a single sentence. "I apologise. We will have it sent now." is not a sufficient response to a client who has listed seven missing items and asked three specific questions. Brief responses to complex messages signal that the client's problem isn't being prioritised, which escalates tone and causes follow-up emails.

### 3. Never asking for the event date in the first reply

A significant number of threads stall because the rep qualified the product but not the deadline. Without the event date, you cannot create legitimate urgency, cannot confirm whether the timeline is feasible, and cannot flag a potential late-delivery risk before it becomes a complaint. Ask for it every time in your first reply.

### 4. Cascading discounts in response to pushback

Some reps respond to a "can you do better?" request by going from 5% to 10%, and then to 15% after a second push. This teaches clients to push. The stronger reps (Ross, Kaviya, Lucus) name their real ceiling once, explain it, and hold it. The threads show that clients who receive a firm but reasonable "this is the best we can do" more often accept than those who sense there's still room to negotiate.

### 5. Confirming delivery dates without checking production capacity

The most damaging complaints in the file — orders that missed events, causing significant client distress and refund demands — all trace back to a rep confirming a delivery date in email without verifying that production could actually meet it. David Johns' "I can't tell you how disappointed I am" (Email 97, Complaints), and the Coaches Congress water-bag emergency (Email 108, Complaints) are both caused by this. Never commit to a specific delivery date without confirming the production slot. Use the formula: "Our standard is 5–7 working days from artwork approval — if we receive your approval by [DATE], you'll receive it by [DATE]." Let the client calculate.

## Section 4: Quick-Reference Card for New Reps

---

### HOW TO OPEN

Address by first name. One warm line. Restate what they asked for in your own words — this proves you read the message. Then answer the question.

*Don't start with:* "Dear Customer," / "Thank you for your enquiry, please find attached..." / A paragraph of product specs before answering anything.

*Do start with:* "Hi [NAME], thanks for reaching out — happy to help with [WHAT THEY ASKED]. Here's the quote: [LINK]"

---

### HOW TO QUALIFY

Ask maximum three questions per email. The three most important are always:

1. **What are the exact dimensions?** (Width × height, or number of panels)
2. **When is the event / what is your delivery deadline?**
3. **Do you have artwork ready, or do you need design help?**

Secondary questions (delivery postcode, frame owned or needed, backlit or standard, shell scheme brand) come after you have these three.

---

### HOW TO QUOTE

Send the invoice link: `backdropsource.co.uk/invoices/[token]`

In the same email, confirm:
- What's included (print only / print + frame / + delivery)
- Delivery timeline: **5–7 working days from artwork approval** (UK standard)
- Any discount applied or code Z5 available (5% off)
- One-line Yes/No close: "Shall we go ahead? Yes/No"

Do not paste a pricing table unless the client has specifically asked for a bulk pricing breakdown.

---

### HOW TO HANDLE A MOCKUP REQUEST

**Artwork in:** Ask for PDF, AI, JPEG, or PNG at 300 DPI minimum. For multiple designs, get a written breakdown of which design goes on which product.

**Mockup out:** Subject line: *"Order No.: EU#XXXXX - Your 2D Mockup is Ready – Please Review and Approve"*. Include delivery timeline reminder and the CMYK colour-shift disclaimer. Always say: once approved, production starts immediately and no further changes are possible.

**If the client requests changes:** "No problem at all — happy to make those changes." Ask for exact colour codes (CMYK) if colour is changing. Confirm what you understood the change to be. Send revised mockup same day or next business day.

**If the client goes quiet on a mockup:** Chase at 2–3 days. Add deadline calculation. Yes/No close.

---

### HOW TO FOLLOW UP

**Day 2–3:** Warm check-in. Recap what they needed. Mention Z5. Yes/No close.

**Day 4–6:** Add urgency — calculate and name the approval deadline needed to hit their event date.

**Day 7–10:** Clear outcome ask. State production timeline. "Ready to proceed, or is there anything you'd like adjusted? Yes/No."

**Day 14+:** Soft final chase. Acknowledge they may have moved on. Leave the door open. "If plans have changed, a quick note either way would be much appreciated."

Never follow up with the same message twice. Each message should add something — a date calculation, a configuration option, a discount offer — that wasn't in the previous one.

---

### HOW TO HANDLE A COMPLAINT

**Step 1:** Acknowledge same day. Apologise without qualification. Don't investigate before apologising.

**Step 2:** Ask for one thing — a photo, a delivery address, bank details — whatever you need to move forward. Not a list of questions.

**Step 3:** Confirm the resolution path: reprint, replacement, or refund. Give a timeline.

**Step 4:** If damage is transit-related, raise a UPS/DHL claim in parallel with dispatching the replacement. Don't make the client wait for the claim outcome.

**Step 5:** For orders over £1,000 or safety issues, escalate to Kaviya or Ross before responding.

**Refund address:** Bpworks INC, C/o DMC Distribution Ltd, Ensor Way, New Mills, High Peak, SK22 4NQ  
**Refund method:** Bank transfer — ask for sort code and account number, or offer PayPal.

## Section 5: The Distinct Communication Style of the UK Market

Based on patterns across 1,368 emails, UK clients communicate in ways that are meaningfully different from what BDS experiences with US clients. Understanding this shapes how to write, what to emphasise, and how to read the temperature of a conversation.

### Tone: Restrained, not effusive

UK clients are polite but rarely gushing. Where a US client might write "This is AMAZING, I love it, you guys are the best!", a UK client writes "Thank you, this looks great." The positive sentiment is the same — the register is just quieter. Reps should match this. Phrases like "We're so excited to work with you!" feel slightly off to a UK reader. "Happy to help" and "Looking forward to working with you" are more natural.

By the same token, UK client frustration is often underplayed. "I'm a bit disappointed" in a UK email can carry the same weight as "I'm furious" from a US client. "Not ideal" often means the client is seriously unhappy. Read the words at face value and you'll underestimate the problem.

### Complaints: Framed as reasonable requests, not demands

UK clients lodge complaints with measured language. They do not typically open with threats or legal references in the first message — they describe the problem, explain the impact, and say what resolution they'd like. This is true even for significant failures. Aaron Jones (Powderkeg, £5,799 order) described his entire multi-issue complaint in calm, professional terms before explaining he couldn't accept the partial refund.

This courtesy should not be mistaken for an easy resolution. UK clients who don't get a satisfactory response will escalate — but they usually give you a reasonable chance first.

### Formality: Lower than expected, but consistent

UK business emails tend to use first names from the first message. "Hi Kaviya" is normal from a new enquirer. There's no formal opening salutation ("Dear Ms Smith") except in complaint escalations or formal refund requests. Sign-offs like "Kind regards," "Many thanks," and "Best," are standard — "Warm regards" is slightly warmer and used when the rep wants to project relationship. The absence of a warm sign-off is not impolite; it's just efficient.

UK clients do not typically use "Cheers" in formal order correspondence (that's more casual/social), but they do use it in WhatsApp exchanges and follow-up threads once a relationship is established.

### Directness: Higher than their tone suggests

UK clients state what they need clearly, and they expect direct answers. If they ask "can you deliver by the 12th?", they want a yes or no — not a paragraph about production capacity. If they ask for the price, they want the number first, context second. The best reps in the threads match this: they answer the question in the first sentence, then elaborate.

Where US client emails often include a lot of context before the question, UK emails tend to lead with the question. Reps who answer it immediately are perceived as competent and responsive. Reps who bury the answer in caveats and qualifications frustrate UK clients quickly.

### Price discussions: Transactional but not aggressive

UK clients negotiate, but they do it with a certain restraint. They rarely make ultimatums in early price discussions ("give me 25% or I go elsewhere") — they ask questions: "Is there any flexibility?" or "Could you do anything on the price?" This is the opening of a negotiation, not a closing statement.

The correct response is a direct, calm counter-offer — not a defensive explanation of why the price is what it is. UK clients respect transparency: "The best I can apply here is 10%; at £900+ I can unlock 15%." They find hedging and vagueness irritating.

US clients tend to be more comfortable with aggressive first anchors and explicit counter-offers. UK clients prefer the same outcome to be reached through a more measured back-and-forth.

### Urgency: Understated until it becomes critical

UK clients often describe time-sensitive needs without flagging the urgency explicitly. "We were hoping to have it by the 15th" might be said in a completely even tone — but if the 15th is an event date, missing it is a serious problem. Reps need to read the event date, calculate the timeline, and flag the risk proactively rather than waiting for the client to say "this is urgent."

By contrast, when a UK client does say "this is urgent," take it at face value and treat it as critical. They have usually already waited longer than they wanted to before using that word.

### WhatsApp: Common and informal, but binding

WhatsApp is used regularly by both reps and clients for quick updates, photo sharing, and informal discussions about spec changes. UK clients treat WhatsApp messages as part of the official record — if a rep promises to follow up on WhatsApp and doesn't, the client notices and references it in their email complaint. The Complaints playbook includes two cases (Joe Chambers, and the Coaches Congress water-bag case) where a failed WhatsApp response became the primary cause of the escalation. Don't make WhatsApp promises you don't intend to keep.

### Reviews: Requested and often delivered

UK clients are generally willing to leave Google and Trustpilot reviews when asked, especially after a positive delivery experience. The key is to ask at the right moment — immediately after delivery, not before the client has seen the product. Direct links to the review pages are necessary; UK clients will not search for BDS on Google to leave a review unless the link is right in front of them.
